What Are "Non-Binding" Propositions?

When you vote in a Texas primary, you'll find a list of propositions at the end of your ballot. Unlike general election propositions (which amend the state constitution), these primary propositions are non-binding‚Äîmeaning your vote won't directly change any laws.

So why bother voting on them? They serve an important purpose in shaping the future direction of each party.

üö´ What "Non-Binding" Means

  • No immediate legal effect ‚Äî Passing a proposition doesn't create, change, or repeal any law
  • Not a constitutional amendment ‚Äî Unlike November ballot measures, these don't amend state or local law
  • Party-specific ‚Äî Each party writes their own propositions; you only see your party's list
  • Binary choice ‚Äî You simply vote YES (agree) or NO (disagree)

‚úÖ What Purpose They Serve

  • Gauge voter priorities ‚Äî Party leaders learn what issues matter most to their base
  • Shape the party platform ‚Äî Results inform the official platform adopted at state conventions
  • Guide legislators ‚Äî Lawmakers use results to prioritize bills in upcoming sessions
  • Signal to candidates ‚Äî Politicians learn what positions are popular with primary voters
üí° Why This Matters More for Republicans: Because the GOP controls all statewide offices and both chambers of the Texas Legislature, Republican proposition results often translate directly into legislative action. As University of Houston political scientist Brandon Rottinghaus noted: "When [Republican] voters pass these referendums, they wind up in the party platform‚Äîbut they also may lead to legislation." Democratic propositions signal priorities but have less immediate legislative impact due to the party's minority status.
